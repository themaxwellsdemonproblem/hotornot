What happens when you grow up?

“[Maxwell's Demon's] B-Sides and Rarities takes place in the world of noise and nakedly honest emotion that pops up during the decade where youthful optimism morphs into a very adult reckoning of the real world […] A hot mess of a debut.” -Coke Machine Glow

"These songs sound like they were recorded in a small room with no doors and a wall completely covered in amps in a three-apartment building across from an auto parts store on a working class street on the edge of the neighborhood.

Put B-sides and Rarities on, and instantly the fuzzy noise pop that wafts out of your speakers will carry you to an era where people fought post-adolescent social anxiety with oblique literary references incorporated into songs played with a guitar tuned to 1992.

All over the country, young, eager and most likely stoned groups of well-intentioned youths are forming bands. They’re scraping together money and buying secondhand guitars and teaching themselves to play the drums on a kit they found in a crumbling barn in the wilds of New Jersey. But only Maxwell’s Demon will lead us all to the promised land, where beer flows like wine and the chorus of angels has been replaced by Archers of Loaf.” -Bailey Somers and Tim Fernholz, 2008

Being teenagers in New Jersey in the 1990s meant we were always looking for something better. We could fill the gas tank in Mom's station wagon with a twenty and hit Rt 17, cross county lines to pick up a bandmate, and stop at Guitar Center to fiddle and dream. "Fake Plastic Trees" and other songs about the suburbs were in the tape deck. We knew New York City. We were raised to believe that the world was ours, and we didn't want it - we wanted something realer. We were smart but nobody is wise at that age.

Track 1: 14-A

So in our first few years of finding our way around a fretboard, Chris's Aunt Patty let us use her barn in Ho-Ho-Kus, New Jersey. On weekends we'd lug amps up the rickety stairs and plonk thin sounds from our new guitars and gingerly thomp on some found drums.

Of course we titled a song after a highway exit - one part accurate title about the inspiration and two-parts thumbing our noses at our classmates from Queens who'd ask "Which exit?" as if they were clever. When we were teens we looked for meaning. We've been trying.

We called ourselves Green. It was the kind of name that we only realize was fitting now that we're deep into our thirties and feeling nostalgic.

So while Matt was away at college, Evan phoned with an absurd proposition: we each buy a mic and a cable, run them into "the little German mixer," and line out to a tape deck: we could record. Now, this was when there was no GarageBand - only garage bands. To cut a demo tape - and to do it yourself? - this was an accomplishment.

Track 2: Big World

Having made it through years spent recording the terrible and long jams of beginners and now exploring our world as songwriters, we took another step. We got better at the drums though none of us was a drummer. Chris abandoned the bass in favor of the guitar and the pen. We found the pitch most of the time.

And so by the time Matt graduated - with Evan and Chris to follow a couple years later - those barn jams and bedroom practice sessions resulted in Signal-to-Noise, fully equipped as a rock band with our own tentative drumming, more distortion, and one less instrumental jam than Fitter/Happier:

Track 3: 90 Years Later

It was the start of the gmail era - archive everything - so we can now find the emails we sent to everybody we knew announcing our first real show - August 2005 at Don Hill's. We still have the video from our half-hour long set. It was raining outside and leaking inside, drips catching the stage lights as Chris's dad toggles between regular and nightvision on the camcorder. That's right - we've got video of the whole thing, and you know what? It's not bad.

Track 4: Life During Wartime

One short year later - or was it two? - Chris signed a lease on an apartment in Bushwick and we moved our amplifiers in. Matt was in the Bronx and Evan was in Jersey City but the point was that we had all coalesced in the same area. Our twenties were fresh and new, our jobs were awful, but for the first time in five or six years (out of about seven as a band, by that point), we were all in the same place again.
