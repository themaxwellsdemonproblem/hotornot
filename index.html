The Maxwell's Demon Problem

Remasters, re-issues, re-living, commentary, and multimedia (2021)

"These songs sound like they were recorded in a small room with no doors and a wall completely covered in amps in a three-apartment building across from an auto parts store on a working class street on the edge of the neighborhood.

Put B-sides and Rarities on, and instantly the fuzzy noise pop that wafts out of your speakers will carry you to an era where people fought post-adolescent social anxiety with oblique literary references incorporated into songs played with a guitar tuned to 1992.

All over the country, young, eager and most likely stoned groups of well-intentioned youths are forming bands. They’re scraping together money and buying secondhand guitars and teaching themselves to play the drums on a kit they found in a crumbling barn in the wilds of New Jersey. But only Maxwell’s Demon will lead us all to the promised land, where beer flows like wine and the chorus of angels has been replaced by Archers of Loaf.” -Bailey Somers and Tim Fernholz, 2008



Track 1: 14A

14A is my exit, and those radio antenna were the ones I’d drive by in my father’s beat up old Buick, an hour past the learner’s permit curfew, on my way back from playing guitar at Chris’ house. They rise out of the otherwise nothingness of the Meadowlands, and they’ve made an appearance in at least one later Le Samourai song. If I remember correctly, someone took a handheld recorder to the escalators of one PATH station or another to get that little sound bed under the intro guitar riff. -ESB

Being teenagers in New Jersey in the 1990s meant we were always looking for something better. I would fill the gas tank in my parents' station wagon with a twenty and cross county lines to pick up Evan, take him to Chris’s, and then we’d go to Guitar Center to noodle and dream. "Fake Plastic Trees" and other songs about the suburbs were in the tape deck. We knew New York City. We were raised to believe that the world was ours, and we didn't want it - we wanted something realer. We were smart, I guess, but nobody is wise at that age. Still, Evan could really dish out some poetry from behind an acoustic guitar: "There must be someone out there trying to talk to us". -MM

This song by Evan holds up the best out of anything from the first era of the band, back when we called ourselves Green (after talking Mattio out of “The Normal”). We tracked this in my bedroom in Glen Rock like so much of the stuff we recorded early in college, after our earliest high school days jamming and four-tracking in the loft of the Barn at Aunt Patty’s place nearby. This was probably the last song where I played bass on record before switching to six-string and drums. I hear this and think of those night drives on the Turnpike and the rest of the awful web of north Jersey highways, of running with guitars slung over shoulders and amps lashed onto rattly handcarts to catch train rides over and under swamps and rivers. We met in high school in New York, and finally moved there years later, but New Jersey is where our music came from, at least in 2004. -CJN

Track 2: Big World

Having made it through years spent recording the terrible and long jams of beginners and learners, now exploring our world as songwriters, we took another step. We got better at the drums though none of us was a drummer. Chris abandoned the bass in favor of the guitar and the pen. We found the pitch - most of the time. And so by the time I graduated from college - with Evan and Chris soon to follow - those barn jams and bedroom practice sessions resulted in Signal-to-Noise, fully equipped as a rock band with our own tentative drumming, more distortion, and one less instrumental jam than Fitter/Happier. “Big World” was a big step forward for us in precision - not the least of which involved the tiny turns of the knob on Chris’s Marshall amp to get the perfect delay timing on my rhythm guitar. -MM

“Big World” wasn’t just the first time I ever played drums on a track: it was practically the first time I ever played drums, period. I had gone back to the Barn, dug up my cousin Brennan’s battered old drum set from his 90s NJ punk/ska band, and dragged it up to my attic room in Glen Rock. We only had a high-hat cymbal, no crash or ride. I had been playing guitar for a while by then but had never been confident in my early attempts at songwriting until this point, and adding drums to the mix seemed like a good idea too. It was the summer of 2005, after sophomore year of college, and I was an intern at Men’s Health Magazine in New York. We spent a weekend in the Glen Rock attic to record the whole Signal to Noise EP, and we were having such a time I called in sick to work Monday with the “stomach flu” so we could finish the job. Upon my return to work, my editor made me write a sidebar piece (ostensibly for the magazine) about why there is technically no such thing as the stomach flu. It is actually called gastroenteritis, a kindly doctor informed me. Whatever you call it, I didn’t have it. -CJN

This is the first song where I remember we were using a big digital multi-track recorder. Still with the now-beat up microphones we bought for the Barn (I last used mine to record lead guitar a year ago - still kicking). This song always makes me think of our sneaking into some now-dead bar or another underage, Matt holding forth about something. This was the era when we realized our guitars would make big loud noises if we let them, and we began to spend a lot of time just letting the texture of the sound run wild -ESB
