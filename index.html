Hot or Not: the Maxwell's Demon Problem

Remasters, re-issues, re-living, commentary, nostalgia, and multimedia, offered to you on the 20th anniversary of our first jam session in the Legendary Portanova Barn, Ho-Ho-Kus, New Jersey

“[Maxwell's Demon's] B-Sides and Rarities takes place in the world of noise and nakedly honest emotion that pops up during the decade where youthful optimism morphs into a very adult reckoning of the real world […] A hot mess of a debut.” -David Abravanel, Coke Machine Glow

Dedicated to Aunt Patty

This project is dedicated to Patricia Anne Norton Portanova and the Portanova family, without whom we would just be three high school friends who jammed sometimes.

The Portanova and Norton families are in our thoughts. We love you, Aunt Patty.



Origins & Myths

I fell in love with music early in life, and while my life as a musician began with the trumpet and the piano, I knew I really wanted to play guitar. Being in a band seemed like lots of fun to pre-teen Chris, and being a rock star would obviously be extremely cool. The chance finally came around the tail end of freshman year of high school in New York City in the year 2000, when Evan and I started hanging out and decided we should start a band. He already played guitar and had recently gotten a Fender Strat that was this deep beautiful purple color, and we knew some other guys who played guitar, so I figured it would be easiest for me to learn the bass and then we'd find a drummer eventually. My Uncle Brian passed down to me an old Fender Jazz Bass copy he had sitting around that went unplayed for years, and resident family guitar wizard Uncle Steve showed me how to make that bass thump the way I wanted. The next year in spring 2001, Evan introduced me to Mattio, who was about to graduate, was already in a band, and lived pretty close to me in Jersey. After an initial afterschool jam in a classroom, we got Matt to come out to my Aunt Patty's place in Ho Ho Kus NJ where she had an old barn out back that was generally gathering dust. We hauled our little amps up to the barn loft and proceeded to make a serious racket for the rest of that summer, and the summer after, and any other time Mattio came home from college. We ran old cassette recorders to record our earliest fumbling attempts at songwriting, and learned how to get sounds on tape in a way that made us happy even if they didn't actually sound very good. The Barn was the physical space we needed to become a real band, the place to play together that every band needs to become what it is. We owe the Portanova family big time for gracing us with that space and gamely putting up with our musical growing pains for all those years.-CJN

So here’s where it started: I think I actually remember what staircase we were in, in high school, when Evan and Chris stopped me and Evan said, “Hey. Wanna come over to Chris’s aunt’s house and jam with us some weekend?” So I’d fill the tank in my parent’s station wagon with a twenty and cross county lines to pick him up, take him to Chris’s, or we’d go to Guitar Center to fiddle and dream. "Fake Plastic Trees" and other songs about the suburbs were in the tape deck. We knew New York City. We were raised to believe that the world was ours, but we wanted something realer than that. We wanted art and we found it in each other, in the barn, and in late-night studio jam sessions in Jersey City with Evan’s dad Mike, a drummer of great timing and patience who was more than happy to lay it down as we learned our instruments. -MM

I grew up in a musical family. My father trained as a jazz drummer in Harlem in his 20’s, and music was always an assumed part of life when I was growing up. When I was 13, he bought me my first guitar, a used Epiphone acoustic, which I learned to play on long-distance phone calls with my cousin in upstate New York. Once I knew my way around and wanted to make more noise, my grandpa helped me buy the electric guitar I wanted - a made-in-Mexico Fender Stratocaster, metallic blue with a rosewood fretboard and a humbucker in the bridge. To date, it’s the only guitar where I’ve worn the frets down to the wood. I got the guitar the same summer I started spending countless weekends at Chris’ house, so the next step was obvious: live the dream, start a band. Chris’ house, and later his Aunt Patty’s barn, would become our creative home for the next seven years. Everyone bought a microphone and a cable. I sprung for a tiny little mixer, and we ran everything into a single-deck tape recorder from the 70s we found covered in cobwebs in Ho-Ho-Kus. There was no tracking. If the mix wasn’t right, we had to take it from the top. We won’t make you listen to those songs, but recording that way requires and forms a bond - and a dedication to making music - that’s hard to shake. -ESB
Track 1: 14A

14A is my exit, and those radio antenna were the ones I’d drive by in my father’s beat up old Buick, an hour past the learner’s permit curfew, on my way back from playing guitar at Chris’ house. They rise out of the otherwise nothingness of the Meadowlands, and they’ve made an appearance in at least one later Le Samourai song. By my memory, someone took a handheld recorder to the escalators of one PATH station or another to get that little sound bed under the intro guitar riff, but Matt and Chris say they recorded a NJ Transit train on its way past Glen Rock. -ESB

This song by Evan holds up the best out of anything from the first era of the band, back when we called ourselves Green (after talking Mattio out of “The Normal”). We tracked this in my bedroom in Glen Rock like so much of the stuff we recorded early in college, after our earliest high school days jamming and recording to cassette in the loft of the Barn at Aunt Patty’s place nearby. This was probably the last song where I played bass on record before switching to six-string and drums. I hear this and think of those night drives on the Turnpike and the rest of the awful web of north Jersey highways, of running with guitars slung over shoulders and amps lashed onto rattly handcarts to catch train rides over and under swamps and rivers. We met in high school in New York, and finally moved there years later, but New Jersey is where our music came from, at least back then circa 2004. -CJN

Once I went away to college we’d email each other staticky versions of songs we wrote and tell the others to come up with parts for the songs. One day Evan called with a proposal: “We each buy a microphone, plug them into my little German mixer, run it into the reel-to-reel tape recorder I found, and we record our own album!” It felt audacious, at the time. We mostly recorded experiments, jams, and our own learning processes. Fitter, Happier, and More Productive was the tightest and most mature set from those years. In hindsight it sounds crazy to compress over three years of musical education into one track but here we are. -MM

Track 2: Big World

Having made it through years spent recording the terrible and long jams of beginners and learners, now exploring our world as songwriters, we took another step. We got better at the drums though none of us was a drummer. Chris abandoned the bass in favor of the guitar and the pen. We found the pitch - most of the time. And so by the time I graduated from college - with Evan and Chris to follow a couple years later - those barn jams and bedroom practice sessions resulted in Signal-to-Noise, fully equipped as a rock band with our own tentative drumming, more distortion, and one less instrumental jam than Fitter/Happier. “Big World” was a big step forward for all of us in precision and timing together - not the least of which involved the tiny turns of the knob on Chris’s Marshall amp to get the perfect delay timing on my rhythm guitar. This one was written for my guy Tom Hutcheon, across the booth at The Cottage Bar in Teaneck, the summer after we finished college. What would come next was uncertain, but Evan and Chris started talking about moving to NYC after graduation, we started thinking over new names for the band, and we started making plans. -MM

“Big World” wasn’t just the first time I ever played drums on a track: it was practically the first time I ever played drums, period. I had gone back to the Barn, dug up my cousin Brennan’s battered old drum set from his 90s NJ punk/ska band, and dragged it up to my attic room in Glen Rock. We only had a high-hat cymbal, no crash or ride. I had been playing guitar for a while by then but had never been confident in my early attempts at songwriting until this point, and adding drums to the mix seemed like a good idea too. It was the summer of 2005, after sophomore year of college, and I was an intern at Men’s Health Magazine in New York. We spent a weekend in the Glen Rock attic to record the whole Signal to Noise EP, and we were having such a time I called in sick to work Monday with the “stomach flu” so we could finish the job. Upon my return to work, my editor made me write a sidebar piece (ostensibly for the magazine) about why there is technically no such thing as the stomach flu. It is actually called gastroenteritis, a kindly doctor informed me. Whatever you call it, I didn’t have it. -CJN

This is the first song where I remember we were using a big digital multi-track recorder. Still with the now-beat up microphones we bought for the Barn (I last used mine to record lead guitar a year ago - still kicking). This song always makes me think of our sneaking into some now-dead bar or another underage, Matt holding forth about something. This was the era when we realized our guitars would make big loud noises if we let them, and we began to spend a lot of time just letting the texture of the sound run wild. -ESB

Track 3: 90 Years Later

I’m still surprised I wrote this song when I did. It’s deeply personal and autobiographical, and traces the difference between the suburbs Chris and Matt grew up in, and the city I grew up in. It’s written in the second person to my great-grandfather, who lived in the same rowhouse my parents live in today. This song also set up a kind of aesthetic I always liked and that served our band well -- me doing something relatively pretty and delicate on the rhythm guitar, and Matt or Chris cranking out loud and rhythmic over it. -ESB

This was my first true guitar freakout on record and I’m still proud as hell of what I pulled off here with nothing more than my forever-out-of-tune Epiphone Sheraton guitar and my janky Marshall solid state amp’s built-in delay effect. At that first club show we ever played at Don Hill’s this summer of 2005, my hometown acquaintance Pat Stickles (who had recently founded the band Titus Andronicus) was in attendance and told me that we sounded like a jazz band on Evan’s songs, an emo band on Mattio’s songs, and an indie rock band on my songs. This is ostensibly one of the “jazz band” songs. It remained in our live repertoire for years longer than most of the other Signal to Noise songs, and it was always fun to rip into this and take off into space. This song consistently let me enjoy the most fun I ever had playing lead guitar in MD. -CJN

It was the start of the gmail era - archive everything - so we can now find the emails we sent to everybody we knew announcing our first real show - August 2005 at Don Hill's. We still have the video from our half-hour long set. It was raining outside and leaking inside, drips catching the stage lights as Chris's dad toggles between regular and nightvision on the camcorder. That's right - we've got video of the whole thing, and you know what? It's not bad. We still recorded this with the learning/jamming attitude (teach me a song in the afternoon and we’ll record it in the evening), so there are a few cues that are still all over the place, but the foundation of rhythm, melody, and noise all coming together was present here and this song stuck around in our live repertoire longer than our other early songs. -MM

Track 4: Life During Wartime

Man I still fucking love this choppy riff. This started as a jam we codenamed “Jeff Tweedy’s Daughter,” and developed over the course of a year or so. I cribbed the Talking Heads name because we were living through a war that didn’t touch us in any way. The lyrics are all about early-twenties sex and bravado, how a few of us were all flirting with and hooking up with the same people, and how the navel-gazing self-consciousness of youth reads as brash indifference. We had fun. For us with our privilege, life during wartime was just, life. This is the era where we actually recorded into a laptop like a real band. I sang this through a tube-overdrive pedal loaded with a prized Telefunken 12AX7 I scored for cheap at a flea market. I remember this being the era where I would drink a bud heavy or three and shout ‘guys lets run this whole mix through a walkman and get that ‘tape’ sound!’ It still burns me that the guy we paid too much to do the original mastering tried his best with our terrible recording techniques, but there are still little stereo artifacts in there. -ESB

A short year or two after “Big World,” Chris had signed a lease on an apartment in Bushwick and we moved our amplifiers in. I was in the Bronx and Evan was in Jersey City but that’s close enough for rock and roll. Our twenties were still fresh and new, our jobs were awful, but for the first time in five or six years (out of about seven as a band, by that point), we were all in the same place again. So we got together over Buds from the bodega across the street from Chris’s place, taught each other the new songs, and started making plans to record a real record.

We - well, Chris, but really all of us - were bad neighbors, but I’ll say this: we turned the amps off at 8pm. We were pretty clueless, but not completely.

“Jeff Tweedy’s Daughter” because it was a mashup of Wilco and Sonic Youth. -MM

By the summer of 2007 Bushwick was on the hipster radar (otherwise our erstwhile bassist Kevin and I never would have moved there) but the DeKalb Ave L train stop at that point had zero gentrifying hipster “infrastructure” and few if any other hipsters yet. The first few months in that shotgun apartment on a block that still bore the scars of arson in the 70s was a real eye-opener for me, fresh out of the collegiate privilege bubble by about one month. I wholeheartedly thought (knew) that New York was the greatest city on earth and I would probably live there til I died and have my ashes scattered in the East River, but who was I kidding? I was totally unprepared for what it was like to live in a working class city neighborhood. As such, it was easy to do totally rude and perhaps even antisocial things like fill an entire closet in one room of said shotgun apartment to the ceiling with a stack of amplifiers, shove in the old drums, play guitars as insanely loud and noisy as you hear on this track, and call it Hangover Studios. We alienated a whole lot of neighbors, made friends of convenience with others, and I’m proud to keep calling a handful of them friends to this day. We were 110% powered by six packs of Budweiser heavy tall boys from the bodega across the street and plentiful cheap weed. I got bedbugs, I had panic attacks, and I had so much fun despite how awful everything was that I didn’t care at all. -CJN

Track 5: Confidence Man

I’m not even sure that’s me on bass in the recording. On stage, though, I’d have Kevin’s blue bass, and he’d dance at me while holding my guitar before his part came in. The switching-of-the-instruments became MD’s one true stage ritual after a while - end a song, and everyone’s taking off a guitar, lowering a mic stand, raising up a drum throne. This remains one of my favorite songs of ours, and it’s the quintessential Chris song for me. -ESB

“Confidence Man” is a song I worked on in fits and starts for years after coming up with the chords. Like a lot of songs I wrote back then, it was about all these feelings and things I thought I knew or wanted, but didn’t realize back then that I didn’t understand or need. Anyway, Confidence Man is the long name for a con man, so just keep that in mind here as you consider my exquisitely complicated lyrical strings of references and allusions, and then consider whether you should listen to me or I’m full of shit. Sometimes I think this is the best song I ever wrote for MD, and once in a while I wonder if it might be the best song I ever wrote, period. It’s a real burner, in large part due to Mattio’s furious drumming. It hits the peak of what I love about 80s/90s indie rock and noise pop. Evan did not learn and has never learned how to play the rhythm guitar line to this song, which means this was basically the one song he played bass on forever and every screaming in-the-red guitar you hear here is me playing my heart right out of my chest. -CJN

I asked Chris what his inspiration for this song was, and he said “Romanticism in the Twentieth Century” which is the kind of answer that made me feel like I should have paid more attention in school, read more books, and understood how it all worked together. But I understood what Chris was doing with his suspended chords, roots, and ringing b and e strings. We played this for a long time, and my drumming kept getting better because I was inspired to keep up with my brilliant boys. I know it’s small, but I’m proud of the crash on the two of the chorus. I have a memory of playing this at Lakeside Lounge - one of the many wonderful small bars and venues we played at that are now gone, victims of skyrocketing rents - and being on full autopilot: the tempo too fast, but completely locked in, as the song just moved through all of us at the same time. -MM

Track 6: Grapefruit

Basically every girl who was on my mind at this point right on the knife edge of college and post-grad “real world” is here and present in “Grapefruit.” I was thinking a lot about girls who weren’t interested in me, not thinking enough about the girls who were, and having a lot of feelings about those extremely worthwhile girls who sometimes were interested in me and sometimes were not. And then there were the guys. To those of you who are in here and reading this: Hi! I still think about you and I probably still love you in some fashion. One time a friend of Matt’s (Jack Crank?) laid down a super sick burn on me while we were ripping into this mid-show in the basement at the Charleston, yet another north Brooklyn dive on our hit list but this one uniquely prominent for serving a free pizza with every beer if you asked for it. He was all like, “What is this, a Police song??” I did not take it well, even though I love Andy Summers’ guitar sound. Seriously though, this song has got hooks for days, if I do say so myself. -CJN

I 100% was thinking of The Police when I suggested we go to half-time for the pre-chorus in this. This song became an anchor of live shows. We’d play at Lakeside Lounge (a stage hooked up by Roscoe, a great guy and true lifelong rock and roller who was a bud through the bike scene, who’d invite the other bike nerds for two-for-one happy hour during the Tour de France recap and let us bring our bikes inside the bar), or Piano’s, or Goodbye Blue Monday, or the Charleston. We got in touch with a “music promoter” who scored us more shows as long as we could get people in the door and buying drinks. Our friends patiently kept showing up - though I can remember one late Tuesday show on the Lower East Side when our only person in the audience for our 11PM set was Evan’s girlfriend. Now wife, for what it’s worth. -MM

Yeah anyone who takes a two-subway trip to a Wednesday-night show is a keeper. This song was a mainstay of the dive-bar years: turn up the gain, fret those power chords, and plan to clean up the pint of beer you’re about to send tumbling from atop your amplifier. Some of my strongest memories from those days are actually about the other bands we tended to share the stage with -- bands with names like the Affected Affections, or Pieces, or Slow/Dynamite; bands whose mp3’s will occasionally show up on an old hard drive of mine and make me smile. It wasn’t quite a scene, but there was a small group of us noise-makers for a year or two there to set up shows with and trade EPs, share drum kits with or loan a ¼” cable too. -ESB

Track 7: Bringing It Down Just To Give It Away

This is a song that still makes me smile, one of the first were I really embraced the “make some noise” approach to lead guitar that we were learning by listening to a *lot* of the Chapel Hill greats in the car on the way to gigs. This is *the* Maxwell’s Demon song of the B-Sides and Rarities era for me. I was always happy to play it on stage. I was always happy to track it. Even today when Chris and I are in a room with guitars together I want to play it. I don’t remember the actual conversation, but at some point while working on this song there was some moment where I asked permission of Chris to just make Archers-of-Loaf-like noises with my guitar rather than write any lead part, and it couldn’t have worked out any better. -ESB

While we never really were able to put a finger on what we were going for (three different songwriters will do that to a band), this song wound up hitting one of the big sweet spots for us: cacophonous guitars backing up a melody you could sing along to, delivering lyrics about flailing around in our early adulthood. -MM

I like to call this song the feel good hit of the summer of 2008, even if almost no one listened to it. A classic CJN mid-tempo indie rock groover with nasty lyrics and a lot of fuzz. I still think about those indie rock shows at McCarren Pool in Williamsburg and all the way-too-cool-for-me girls there. This was probably the closest we got to that 90s Chapel Hill sound I was so often going for. I wonder if we had released this song (the whole debut full-length, really) like five to ten years later, once 80s/90s indie rock became trendy influences again for the kids, if anything would have been any different. Probably not. But when the kids can write pop songs these days with titles like “I Wish I Was Stephen Malkmus”, heck why not? -CJN

Track 8: Fireproof Safe / Tin Airplanes

It’s either a blessing or a curse to have three songwriters, and as we careened between Evan’s moody jazz rock and Chris’s chunky noise pop I groped around for a sound to call my own. Not a stretch to say it’s a metaphor for my mid twenties. I’m not sure it was a great idea to write a sixty second song about loneliness, insist we record it to my exacting specifications, and never really learn to play it as a band… but my idea to feedback right in to the intro of Tin Airplanes was one of my few inspired recording decisions for B-Sides and Rarities. -MM

Fireproof Safe is one of my favorites. My part was mostly just the aforementioned shouts about making things lo-fi and “tapey,” but it really perfectly captures the best of what we were trying to do. Tin Airplanes was a little jam I’d written in drop-D, trying to marry a bluesy riff with noise. It’s not my strongest effort, but the decision to pair it with Fireproof Safe was a good one, and listening back to the whole thing now, there’s a bit of a Sparklehorse flavor to it that I dig. -MM

I spent a lot of time bullying us into super lo-fi recording processes out of some misguided fealty to a notion of DIY indie rock that was largely disconnected from the reality of the late Aughts. We were recording on a laptop with Pro Tools! What was I thinking? But we did experiment with some cool production techniques here and there regardless, and the combo of Fireproof Safe into Tin Airplanes was the best experimental result on the album. Evan’s half is a real crawling king snake of a swamp jam, and when we played live I would get loud as hell on these lead lines. -CJN

Track 9: Raleigh

A breakup song. I wrote this in between assignments at my first newspaper job. Four-on-the-floor rhythm, no words in the chorus, Chris getting noisy on the lead--we were listening to a lot of Archers of Loaf, a lot of the Chapel Hill scene bands, and it was fitting for this song about a heartbreak in the Research Triangle. I tried to write a slower, twangier version of this later in my Otsego days, but to quote some R.E.M. liner notes, this version crushes that one like a grape. -ESB

So we recorded our album and got tired of gigging it and road tripped separately and got fired or broken-up with or priced out of the neighborhood. We got into the occasional knock-down, grudge-holding argument, and we came back together to work on a new set of songs. There was more distortion, more delay. Chris and I both got a lot better at drumming and Evan started writing rock songs like Raleigh. We thought harder about the transitions in our songs - we wrote tiny pieces of them, just really writing the shit out of how small parts fit together - and they became some of the best damn songs we never recorded. We got tight and strong in our live shows. Kevin didn’t show up to one of them and suddenly we were a three-piece for a little while, until Jean-Claude learned bass and backup vox for all of our songs (and did an amazing delivery of Superstition for our annual New Years’ covers set). But all that’s left of that magical year is a few CDs of sets recorded off the soundboard (one is strong; another, a true trainwreck of a gig), a bad taste left in our mouths from so-called music promoters who let you do all the promotion but took a cut from the door, and the memory of a lot of good bars that aren’t around anymore in neighborhoods we can no longer afford. -MM

I remember playing this tune live onstage at Piano’s on the Lower East Side the night we recorded this and the rest of the songs-for-a-second-album that never made it into the studio. I forgot to turn up my guitar at first and I thought it was gonna wreck the cut because I couldn’t get the balance right, but it didn’t matter because Matt was so insane on the kit for this song. We had gotten a lot tighter and a lot better at each of our instruments by then, especially the drums, and our live shows were really showing it even though our local venues and bars were already starting to close by then due to the changing winds in the city. We never captured our live power in the studio, but I’m glad we have a document of our concert presence, which had gotten massive even though the end of the band was already starting to loom in the lyrics to these songs. Kevin had disappeared, and Jean-Claude picked up the bass to become our favorite fourth member. Hangover Studios gave way briefly to a practice space in Astoria and then to the legendary Castle Asshole when I moved into Bushwick’s notoriously disgusting McKibbin Lofts with the guys in my metal band Predator Raw. Everything in life was getting grosser by the day. -CJN

Track 10: Fuck This Job

If I had a dollar for every time I’ve sung this chorus in my head, I wouldn’t need any of the jobs that called it forth. It was in my head when I made $500 a month running a soundboard. It was in my head when I got paid under the table to work the cash register at a bicycle shop. More recently, a couple of multi-national corporate employers have caused me to hum along as well. This is one of those songs that I feel like I was still working on when we stopped playing out. My part was 80% of the way there. Chris’ rhythm was great, and he and Matt really locked in. One of the things I miss the most about playing music out live is how every song constantly changes, evolves, and grows, as you write it together anew on stage. -ESB

Every now and then I thought that Chris’s lyrics could be too blunt. But after getting fired from one job, having another not renew a contract, scrounging part time jobs through a recession or two, delivering food on my bike through two nasty winters to lower Manhattan elite who didn’t tip for shit, I realized that there’s artistry, not artlessness, in being blunt. I tried to put as much fury into my drumming as Chris put into his words, while Evan anchored the chaos with a really cool rhythmic lead line. -MM

The game was getting pretty raw for young broke people in New York by 2009. I was laid off from my financial due diligence “private investigation” job in October 2008 early in the economic crash and remained out of work for nine long months, during which time I tried and failed to write a book, had a mental breakdown, and barely kept it together until I landed a job as a reporter the following summer (thanks again Bailey). And even after I got that job I barely kept it together. This song is about the job I got laid off from, and all the jobs we all hated we had to have in the first place given that all the jobs we thought we’d get were long gone. Things were hard and sometimes ugly for young working-age people in the Great Recession, especially when piles of student debt were involved. But it was a beautiful time even though a lot of it hurt, and I tried to catch all of that in this song. -CJN

Track 11: Karen

I love this song. I love playing this song. Chris’ mom was practically my second mom for all the weekends I spent holed up in their attic with a guitar in high school, then on breaks from college, then sometimes just a fun visit after college. Karen let us make a ruckus and a mess in her house, and was still somehow always happy to see us, and I am still very happy when I get to see her. To add a few notes to an ode to her was always a privilege. Add to that the fact that Chris’ lyrics just captured perfectly that odd feeling of starting an adult life at home, conceptually distant but geographically close to the people who raised you -- that even after returning home, we can’t go home again -- and this is a song that still makes me misty-eyed to play. -ESB

Real talk, Chris’s honesty and vulnerability and sweetness in a song to his mom made me want to be a better family member and pay better attention to the people in my life. -MM

I wrote this song for my mom for her 50th birthday. Things were tough mentally, professionally, and otherwise in those days for me, and a lot of those tough times are represented in these lyrics. But this song tries to put the tough stuff in perspective, since there’s no other way to grow up and stay alive. -CJN

Track 12: Love Song for Liam

One of my all-time favorite songs I’ve ever been a part of. Every memory I have of this song involves sidling up to the mic next to mattio, belting out backup vocals while trying to keep up for dear life with my right hand. As I remember it, this was the first song of a new, more confident style of writing, where the main songwriter (Matt in this case) would bring what he had to the group, we would write parts around it, and then he would write a *new* part to replace the original. I don’t remember if that’s exactly how this played out but it’s a method I’ve used a lot since. When we played it live we played it fast, and sometimes, when we hit that outro just right, with Matt strumming furiously, it felt like maybe, just maybe, we could be opening for Mission of Burma in the Signals, Calls and Marches era. I feel like this was a song where we’d really matured as a band, and it’s a regret of mine that we never properly tracked this one. -ESB

Liam was a traveler I met who had set out to walk across the country. We had a few friends in common. The last I heard of him, a friend saw him in jail in NYC during the RNC. He cut his walk short - he’d gotten lonely. I’m still not entirely sure what he was looking for, but at the time I wrote the song some years later, I knew what it meant to be looking for something. But a bunch of years past graduation, burning through jobs at an unsustainable rate, constantly cash-strapped, and staring down a year of recession, I didn’t know what there was to hope for and worried that everything we leaned on - yeah, even the music - just wasn’t going to get us through. -MM

We were at the peak of our power as a live trio by the time Matt wrote this tune, and it became our most locked-in groove of the late era of MD. I was finally ok at drums and I felt like a well-oiled murderous robot when we played this one live. There’s definitely some other influences creeping into the band by the time you hear these later tracks, beyond just traditionally noisy indie rock. There’s a sense of rhythm and interplay we didn’t have before, something we picked up over time from other bands we loved like Spoon who we weren’t good enough to successfully imitate until we were almost done as a band. I tried to play drums like my name was Jim Eno, but I wound up sounding like Sam Fogarino. -CJN

Track 13: Vampires and Bonedogs

It took me ten fucking years to find the humbucker on my guitar. -ESB

Early in my experience as a worker, after the thrill of the first few paychecks went away, I’d start to get the sneaking suspicion that I was being exploited - that I was giving away my brain for eight hours a day to somebody under no obligation to return it in one piece. This song is about the terribly stupid anti-terrorism emergency evacuation procedures we got at a job in a building near the Empire State Building, which was as much a part of working in NYC in the Aughts as active shooter drills in schools a decade later. One thing I’ve realized about getting older is that in life, I wind up learning the same lessons over and over, with new depth that comes with repetition. The last year or two we spent as a proper band we stumbled toward writing what’s essentially grunge music, I think, and after spending our early twenties trying to get a leg up in a world that was just starting to pinball from one crisis to another, we returned to what we learned at kids from the suburbs in the 1990s: success sucks and is dumb, chunky guitars and heartbeat drums are good. -MM

Matt was at the top of his game writing this song, and the combo of his lyrics and vocal delivery here are all-time tops for me. He captured the raw awfulness of so much of that era’s economy in our generation’s early working adulthood. This is another one where I felt like I was finally getting where I could do interesting things on the drums, and the build of this tune to the crashing end was a showstopper in concert. This song and many of the other Live at Piano’s tracks captured the nasty vibes of the city that shut us down in the end and led us off to other adventures. By the time we played our farewell show at the Lakeside Lounge in the summer of 2010, we’d been playing guitars together for nearly a decade, from our very first baby steps in the months before 9/11 all the way through adolescence and whatever’s after it until the Tea Party took over Congress. You can hear why it was all ending in the lyrics we were all writing by then. From the very first jam we always wanted something more than the bullshit we were sold, but we didn’t find it in that decade. What we did find were what we now like to call the “garbage days”: a lot of really good times we didn’t always appreciate when they were happening, because, after all, they were happening to us. Those garbage days might have been a hot mess, but our trash fires looked gorgeous as they burned. -CJN

Track 14: Bus Stop

This song got lost in our catalog. It wasn’t guitar-based. No amplifier threatened to blow a fuse when you played it. While everything else we were playing out was meant to sizzle, this was meant to smolder. I was also deeply uncomfortable about singing in those days, and this vocal take took a while. But when I listen to it now, I love it. This was like Love Song for Liam -- the rare song where by the end of it the only thing left from the demo were the lyrics and maybe some of the chord progression. We truly wrote this collaboratively, tearing down what I came in with and building it anew. Since we never really played it out, my memories of it are all from writing it with Matt and Chris and Kevin. And because of that -- and because it didn’t fit neatly into any of what we were playing out at that point -- it reminds me for all the world of the earliest days in the band, young teens gathered around a single-deck cassette-tape recorder in a barn in Ho-Ho-Kus, all of us looking at each other quizzically as the sustain dies off as if to say, “We’re doing this, right?” -ESB

I distinctly remember Evan bringing this song to a session of ours down in DC, and how we all took Evan’s blues riff and cut it to pieces together, with Chris working the synth, Kevin on keys, and me tapping harmonics. Weeks later Chris sent us an MP3 - he’d sat through most of the song before bringing in the tick tock of the drum, a goddamn brilliant bit of patience, and the whole thing just feels like a beautiful rainy night to me. Do young people always know that they’re planting nostalgia for their future selves? I think we played this once, at Wesleyan University, in the graffiti’ed basement of Westco. I only know because Evan’s standing at the mic alone and I’m sitting on the floor. -MM

This is the truest b-side of a band that stupidly decided to call its debut album “B-Sides and Rarities”, only to have that title ring depressingly true over a decade later. We’ve got nothing but rarities at this point, but Bus Stop is a particularly rare tropical bird in the menagerie. Evan stirred up some spooky vibes and we made something unlike any other tune in our catalog. This recording was my final project for an audio engineering class senior year of college. I got an A. -CJN

Track 15: Foursquare

I learned to play guitar hanging out in the attic bedroom of my high school best friend. So much of my experience of playing music -- and all the ways it has enriched my life since Maxwell’s Demon called it quits -- can be traced back to just being a kid hanging out with my friends. And so this goofy song about watching younger kids playing from the window across the street has an honesty that all the other wrought-and-overwrought songs we wrote can’t touch. This is another rare occurrence of me picking up the bass guitar, which is still something I only do in the presence of friends who are willing to let some errant notes slide. -ESB

Our friend Laura paid about as much attention to our band - and to us as people - as anybody in our lives, especially in that turbulent part of our early 20s when we felt confused and alone no matter how many wonderful people we surrounded ourselves with. She called this “the only truly happy song that Maxwell’s Demon wrote.” Which only gets truer in hindsight when we revisit the overwhelming nostalgia written into this song a decade and a half later, to think of the times we had together as a band, to worry that they’re behind us, and the final kicker, to realize that we never really did this song justice in the studio or stage. We played it out some, but did we know what a banger we had on our hands? But we still have each other. And I think at the end of the day, pulling together these stories and these tracks were - like being in the band in the first place - as much an excuse for us to spend time together as it was a means to make music. It’s all the same thing, anyway. -MM

Much credit is due here to MD loyalists Katie and Elizabeth Norton, who contributed mightily to the overarching vision of this song as we watched our neighbors across the street from our bedroom windows and goofed around. Some of the earliest versions I have of these lyrics are written in Katie’s hand, even. Even though the recorded version has never seen the light of day until now, Foursquare has always been a live fan favorite, goofy as it may be, probably because it’s such a naively straightforward charmer that it walks right in your front door before you know it. To Matt and I, this was a natural pick to repurpose as a parody version for Evan’s wedding many years later. Later that wedding night, I toasted Evan and Lauren with a reference to our years together in this very band, of love and music and learning new languages together through shared lives. No other band has come close to the musical relationships I forged with these guys in MD because you don’t get another shot at being young and dumb together, too ignorant and arrogant to know better. “Yes,” we’d say, “we don’t know what we’re doing, but we do it better than you.” -CJN

Appendix A: Where are they now?
CJN

Chris has been writing, recording, and releasing music on a solo basis under his initials CJN since 2017. Nowadays he leans toward fat beats and lush synths, but the guitars are still just as loud as back then. You can also find him livestreaming home studio jams on Twitch on the occasional Saturday afternoon.

The Real Mattio

After taking many years away from music to race bicycles with no brakes on steeply inclined surfaces all over the country (and achieving, perhaps, a level of success in bike racing that's hard to come by in music), Matt now makes lo-fi guitar rock music into a computer in Brooklyn, NY, while trying not to annoy the neighbors.

ESB

Evan continues to gig and record with the alt-country outfit Otsego, which sounds like a punk rocker with a pocket full of tattered Kerouac driving a rattling pickup truck too fast down a dirt road toward the city.

...and notable companions

Throughout its time MD would be nothing if not for those we've played music with and for. We're not sure where all of you are and could not exhaustively list you but you're in our thoughts - this awful year more than ever.
