The Maxwell's Demon Problem

Remasters, re-issues, re-living, commentary, and multimedia, offered to you on the 20th anniversary of our first jam session in the Legendary Portanova Barn, Ho-Ho-Kus, New Jersey

“[Maxwell's Demon's] B-Sides and Rarities takes place in the world of noise and nakedly honest emotion that pops up during the decade where youthful optimism morphs into a very adult reckoning of the real world […] A hot mess of a debut.” -David Abravanel, Coke Machine Glow

Dedicated to Aunt Patty

This project is dedicated to Patricia Anne Norton Portanova and the Portanova family, without whom we would just be three high school friends who jammed sometimes.

The Portanova and Norton families are in our thoughts. We love you, Aunt Patty.



Origins & Myths

I fell in love with music early in life, and while my life as a musician began with the trumpet and the piano, I knew I really wanted to play guitar. Being in a band seemed like lots of fun to pre-teen Chris, and being a rock star would obviously be extremely cool. The chance finally came around the tail end of freshman year of high school in New York City in the year 2000, when Evan and I started hanging out and decided we should start a band. He already played guitar and had recently gotten a Fender Strat that was this deep beautiful purple color, and we knew some other guys who played guitar, so I figured it would be easiest for me to learn the bass and then we'd find a drummer eventually. My Uncle Brian passed down to me an old Fender Jazz Bass copy he had sitting around that went unplayed for years. The next year in spring 2001, Evan introduced me to Mattio, who was about to graduate, was already in a band, and lived pretty close to me in Jersey. After an initial afterschool jam in a classroom, we got Matt to come out to my Aunt Patty's place in Ho Ho Kus NJ where she had an old barn out back that was generally gathering dust. We hauled our little amps up to the barn loft and proceeded to make a serious racket for the rest of that summer, and the summer after, and any other time Mattio came home from college. We ran old cassette recorders and four tracks to record our earliest fumbling attempts at songwriting, and learned how to get sounds on tape in a way that made us happy even if they didn't actually sound very good. The Barn was the physical space we needed to become a real band, the place to play together that every band needs to become what it is. We owe the Portanova family big time for gracing us with that space and gamely putting up with our musical growing pains for all those years.-CJN

So here’s where it started: I think I actually remember what staircase we were in, in high school, when Evan and Chris stopped me and Evan said, “Hey. Wanna come over to Chris’s aunt’s house and jam with us some weekend?” So I’d fill the tank in my parent’s station wagon with a twenty and cross county lines to pick him up, take him to Chris’s, or we’d go to Guitar Center to fiddle and dream. "Fake Plastic Trees" and other songs about the suburbs were in the tape deck. We knew New York City. We were raised to believe that the world was ours, but we wanted something realer than that. We wanted art and we found it in each other, in the barn, and in late-night studio jam sessions in Jersey City with Evan’s dad Mike, a drummer of great timing and patience who was more than happy to lay it down as we learned our instruments. -MM

Track 1: 14A

14A is my exit, and those radio antenna were the ones I’d drive by in my father’s beat up old Buick, an hour past the learner’s permit curfew, on my way back from playing guitar at Chris’ house. They rise out of the otherwise nothingness of the Meadowlands, and they’ve made an appearance in at least one later Le Samourai song. If I remember correctly, someone took a handheld recorder to the escalators of one PATH station or another to get that little sound bed under the intro guitar riff. -ESB

This song by Evan holds up the best out of anything from the first era of the band, back when we called ourselves Green (after talking Mattio out of “The Normal”). We tracked this in my bedroom in Glen Rock like so much of the stuff we recorded early in college, after our earliest high school days jamming and four-tracking in the loft of the Barn at Aunt Patty’s place nearby. This was probably the last song where I played bass on record before switching to six-string and drums. I hear this and think of those night drives on the Turnpike and the rest of the awful web of north Jersey highways, of running with guitars slung over shoulders and amps lashed onto rattly handcarts to catch train rides over and under swamps and rivers. We met in high school in New York, and finally moved there years later, but New Jersey is where our music came from, at least in 2004. -CJN

Being teenagers in New Jersey in the 1990s meant we were always looking for something better. I would fill the gas tank in my parents' station wagon with a twenty and cross county lines to pick up Evan, take him to Chris’s, and then we’d go to Guitar Center to noodle and dream. "Fake Plastic Trees" and other songs about the suburbs were in the tape deck. We knew New York City. We were raised to believe that the world was ours, and we didn't want it - we wanted something realer. We were smart, I guess, but nobody is wise at that age. Still, Evan could really dish out some poetry from behind an acoustic guitar: "There must be someone out there trying to talk to us". -MM

Track 2: Big World

Having made it through years spent recording the terrible and long jams of beginners and learners, now exploring our world as songwriters, we took another step. We got better at the drums though none of us was a drummer. Chris abandoned the bass in favor of the guitar and the pen. We found the pitch - most of the time. And so by the time I graduated from college - with Evan and Chris soon to follow - those barn jams and bedroom practice sessions resulted in Signal-to-Noise, fully equipped as a rock band with our own tentative drumming, more distortion, and one less instrumental jam than Fitter/Happier. “Big World” was a big step forward for us in precision - not the least of which involved the tiny turns of the knob on Chris’s Marshall amp to get the perfect delay timing on my rhythm guitar. -MM

“Big World” wasn’t just the first time I ever played drums on a track: it was practically the first time I ever played drums, period. I had gone back to the Barn, dug up my cousin Brennan’s battered old drum set from his 90s NJ punk/ska band, and dragged it up to my attic room in Glen Rock. We only had a high-hat cymbal, no crash or ride. I had been playing guitar for a while by then but had never been confident in my early attempts at songwriting until this point, and adding drums to the mix seemed like a good idea too. It was the summer of 2005, after sophomore year of college, and I was an intern at Men’s Health Magazine in New York. We spent a weekend in the Glen Rock attic to record the whole Signal to Noise EP, and we were having such a time I called in sick to work Monday with the “stomach flu” so we could finish the job. Upon my return to work, my editor made me write a sidebar piece (ostensibly for the magazine) about why there is technically no such thing as the stomach flu. It is actually called gastroenteritis, a kindly doctor informed me. Whatever you call it, I didn’t have it. -CJN

This is the first song where I remember we were using a big digital multi-track recorder. Still with the now-beat up microphones we bought for the Barn (I last used mine to record lead guitar a year ago - still kicking). This song always makes me think of our sneaking into some now-dead bar or another underage, Matt holding forth about something. This was the era when we realized our guitars would make big loud noises if we let them, and we began to spend a lot of time just letting the texture of the sound run wild -ESB

Track 3: 90 Years Later

I’m still surprised I wrote this song when I did. It’s deeply personal and autobiographical, and traces the difference between the suburbs Chris and Matt grew up in, and the city I grew up in. It’s written in the second person to my great-grandfather, who lived in the same rowhouse my parents live in today. This song also set up a kind of aesthetic I always liked and that served our band well -- me doing something relatively pretty and delicate on the rhythm guitar, and Matt or Chris cranking out loud and rhythmic over it. -ESB

This was my first true guitar freakout on record and I’m still proud as hell of what I pulled off here with nothing more than my forever-out-of-tune Epiphone Sheraton jazz guitar and my janky Marshall solid state amp’s built-in delay effect. At that first club show we ever played at Don Hill’s this summer of 2005, my hometown acquaintance Pat Stickles (who had recently founded the band Titus Andronicus) was in attendance and told me that we sounded like a jazz band on Evan’s songs, an emo band on Mattio’s songs, and an indie rock band on my songs. This is ostensibly one of the “jazz band” songs. It remained in our live repertoire for years longer than most of the other Signal to Noise songs, and it was always fun to rip into this and take off into space. This song consistently let me enjoy the most fun I ever had playing lead guitar in MD. -CJN

It was the start of the gmail era - archive everything - so we can now find the emails we sent to everybody we knew announcing our first real show - August 2005 at Don Hill's. We still have the video from our half-hour long set. It was raining outside and leaking inside, drips catching the stage lights as Chris's dad toggles between regular and nightvision on the camcorder. That's right - we've got video of the whole thing, and you know what? It's not bad. We still recorded this with the learning/jamming attitude (teach me a song in the afternoon and we’ll record it in the evening), so there are a few cues that are still all over the place, but the foundation of rhythm, melody, and noise all coming together was present here and this song stuck around in our live repertoire longer than our other early songs. -MM

Track 4: Life During Wartime

Man I still fucking love this choppy riff. This started as a jam we codenamed “Jeff Tweedy’s Daughter,” and developed over the course of a year or so. I cribbed the Talking Heads name because we were living through a war that didn’t touch us in any way. The lyrics are all about early-twenties sex and bravado, how a few of us were all flirting with and hooking up with the same people, and how the navel-gazing self-consciousness of youth reads as brash indifference. We had fun. For us with our privilege, life during wartime was just, life. This is the era where we actually recorded into a laptop like a real band. I sang this through a tube-overdrive pedal loaded with a prized Telefunken 12AX7 I scored for cheap at a flea market. I remember this being the era where I would drink a bud heavy or three and shout ‘guys lets run this whole mix through a walkman and get that ‘tape’ sound!’ It still burns me that the guy we paid too much to do the original mastering tried his best with our terrible recording techniques, but there are still little stereo artifacts in there. -ESB

A short year or two after “Big World,” Chris had signed a lease on an apartment in Bushwick and we moved our amplifiers in. I was in the Bronx and Evan was in Jersey City but that’s close enough for rock and roll. Our twenties were still fresh and new, our jobs were awful, but for the first time in five or six years (out of about seven as a band, by that point), we were all in the same place again. So we got together over Buds from the bodega across the street from Chris’s place, taught each other the new songs, and started making plans to record a real record.

We - well, Chris, but really all of us - were bad neighbors, but I’ll say this: we turned the amps off at 8pm. We were pretty clueless, but not completely.

“Jeff Tweedy’s Daughter” because it was a mashup of Wilco and Sonic Youth. -MM

By the summer of 2007 Bushwick was on the hipster radar (otherwise our erstwhile bassist Dugan and I never would have moved there) but the Dekalb Ave L stop at that point had zero gentrifying hipster “infrastructure” and not really any other hipsters yet. The first few months in that shotgun apartment on a block that still bore the scars of arson in the 70s was a real eye-opener for old Chris Norton, who was fresh out of the collegiate privilege bubble by about one month. I wholeheartedly thought/knew New York was the greatest city on earth and i would probably live there til I died and have my ashes scattered in the East River, but who was I kidding? I was totally unprepared for what it was like to live in a working class city neighborhood. As such, it was easy to do totally rude and perhaps even antisocial things like fill an entire closet in one room of said shotgun apartment to the ceiling with a stack of amplifiers, shove in the old drums, play guitars as insanely loud and noisy as you hear on this track, and call it Hangover Studios. We alienated a whole lot of neighbors, made friends of convenience with others, and I’m proud to keep calling a handful of them friends to this day. We were 110% powered by six packs of Budweiser heavy tall boys from the bodega across the street and plentiful cheap weed. I got bedbugs, I had panic attacks, and I had so much fun despite how awful everything was that I didn’t care at all. -CJN
